Literature Review

Research context for prescription drug affordability, coupon platforms, and patient access

Introduction

The literature related to this project focuses on prescription drug affordability, patient access barriers, the structure of drug pricing in the United States, and the growing importance of discount platforms such as TrumpRx, GoodRx, SingleCare, and Cost Plus Drugs.

My original proposal asked a broader question about multiple coupon sites and whether TrumpRx was truly transformative. As the project developed, I narrowed the research question to a more focused comparison of selected Type 2 diabetes medications across platforms in terms of price, availability, and transparency. The literature still supports that narrower question because many of the sources show that access is not only about cost, but also about how clearly pricing information is communicated to patients.

Prescription Drug Pricing as a Structural Problem

Several of the sources in my bibliography make it clear that prescription drug pricing in the United States is already difficult to understand before discount platforms are even added into the picture. Robert H. Shmerling’s article from Harvard Health, "Why Do Your Prescription Drugs Cost so Much?" explains that drug prices are shaped by a complicated network of manufacturers, insurers, pharmacy benefit managers, and pharmacies. That source was important because it pushed me to think of these platforms not as simple savings tools, but as websites operating within a much larger and more complicated pricing system.

This broader context matters because it helps explain why discount platforms are attractive to patients in the first place. If the original pricing system were transparent and easy to navigate, there would be much less need for multiple coupon and discount websites. In that sense, these platforms exist because patients are often left trying to assemble access on their own.

Patient Access and Affordability

The 2025 State of Patient Access Report from the PAN Foundation was one of the most useful sources for understanding why this topic matters. The report shows that affordability and access remain serious challenges even for insured patients, which reinforces the idea that discount platforms are not only relevant to uninsured people.

This source also helped shape one of the final directions of my project. It made it clear that a low listed price is only one part of access. If users cannot tell whether they qualify for the discount, what strength is being priced, or what restrictions apply, then the platform may still be difficult to use. That idea directly influenced my decision to include transparency as part of the final comparison.

Discount Platforms and TrumpRx

Several sources in my bibliography provide summaries or comparisons of TrumpRx and related discount platforms. The White House fact sheet on TrumpRx presents the platform in a highly favorable way, emphasizing lower prices and broad benefit to American patients. That source was useful because it provided the official framing of the program, but it also required critical caution because it functions as government messaging rather than neutral analysis.

Other sources such as GovFacts, Nurse.org, and HealthCompiler offered broader comparisons between TrumpRx and other sites like GoodRx or Cost Plus Drugs. These sources were especially helpful because they identified categories of comparison such as pricing model, eligibility, transparency, and access. However, many of them remained fairly general and did not consistently walk through specific drug-level comparisons across platforms.

What the Literature Does Not Fully Answer

The literature explains why prescription affordability is a major problem and why discount platforms matter, but it does not fully answer the most practical comparison question that a user might have. For a specific medication, which site lists it, what price does it show, and how clearly does it explain that price?

This gap became the motivation for the final version of my project. Rather than offering another broad summary of the platforms, I focused on a selected set of Type 2 diabetes medications and compared the sites directly in terms of listed price, availability, and transparency. That narrower focus makes the project more concrete and more useful.

How My Project Fits into the Existing Conversation

My final project fits into this conversation by taking a more focused and user-centered approach than the one I originally proposed. Instead of attempting a broad comparison across a very large catalog of drugs, I limited the project to selected Type 2 diabetes medications and used that narrower scope to make a clearer comparison.

The literature supports that shift. The patient access sources show that affordability remains a major issue. The platform sources show that multiple options exist, but they differ in both design and communication. My project brings those strands together by asking not only where prices appear lower, but also how clearly those prices are communicated to patients trying to make actual decisions.
