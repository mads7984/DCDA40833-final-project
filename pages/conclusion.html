Conclusion

What this project answers, what it contributes, and what questions remain

Conclusion

This project does answer the final research question in a focused and practical way. By comparing selected Type 2 diabetes medications across TrumpRx, GoodRx, SingleCare, and Cost Plus Drugs, the project shows that platform value depends on more than just posted price.

The findings show that price, availability, and transparency all shape whether a platform is actually useful. A site may list a very low price, but if the drug is missing from the platform entirely or the pricing details are unclear, the platform may still fall short as an access tool.

What This Project Contributes

One of the main contributions of this project is that it moves the discussion away from general claims about savings and toward a more concrete comparison of specific drugs across multiple platforms. It also treats transparency as part of the comparison rather than assuming that price alone is enough.

That matters because patients do not just need low prices. They need information they can understand and actually use. Looking at transparency alongside price and availability made the comparison more realistic and more useful.

What the Project Suggests about TrumpRx

One of my original questions was whether TrumpRx was truly transformative. I think the final version of the project gives a more grounded way to approach that question. Rather than trying to prove political motivation directly, the project evaluates the platform based on what it actually presents to users. In that sense, the results suggest that TrumpRx may provide meaningful savings for some drugs, but that does not automatically make it broadly transformative across all medications or all user situations.

For some drugs in the dataset, TrumpRx offered very low listed prices. At the same time, its coverage was limited compared to other platforms, and the details behind those prices were not always as straightforward as the headline number might suggest. That means the platform should be evaluated not only by individual low prices, but also by consistency, access, and clarity.

Limitations

This project has several limitations. The most obvious is scope. I focused on a selected set of Type 2 diabetes medications rather than a larger catalog of drugs. That means the findings should not be treated as a complete statement about every medication on every platform.

Another limitation is that listed prices are not always the same thing as the final amount a user will pay. Pricing can depend on eligibility, pharmacy-specific conditions, manufacturer requirements, or other restrictions that are not fully visible at the first stage of access. I addressed part of this issue by including transparency measures, but the problem itself is larger than what one project can fully capture.

Questions for Future Research

This project raises several new questions. One is how these comparisons would change if the dataset included more medications or more therapeutic categories. Another is how prices would vary by location or pharmacy when a platform gives multiple options. A third question is whether transparency could be measured even more fully by incorporating user testing rather than only evaluating what is visible on the page.

I also think there is room for future work that compares how these platforms function for different kinds of users, such as uninsured patients, commercially insured patients, or users enrolled in government programs. The more I worked on this project, the more I realized that access is not only about posted price, but about whether a specific person can actually use the price being advertised.

Final Reflection

Even though this project ended up narrower than my original proposal, I think that change made the final result stronger. The process taught me that a well-defined scope is not a weakness. It is often what makes meaningful comparison possible. By focusing on selected medications and combining price, availability, and transparency, I was able to create a project that is more focused, more usable, and easier to communicate.

More than anything, this project reinforced how difficult prescription affordability can be to navigate from the patient perspective. If users need multiple platforms just to figure out where a medication is listed, what it costs, and whether they qualify for the advertised savings, then the need for clearer comparison tools is still very real. That is the larger conversation this project contributes to.
